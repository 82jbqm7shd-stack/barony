History of the Barony of Balvill

From Crown charter in 1630 through nearly four centuries of the Campbell dynasty, the abolition of feudal tenure, and into the modern era—the story of a Scottish baronial dignity across the centuries.

Introduction

The Barony of Balvill stands as a testament to nearly four centuries of Scottish legal and social history. Created in 1630 during the reign of Charles I, confirmed by Act of Parliament in 1633, and held continuously by the Campbell family and the Dukes of Argyll until 2025, this feudal barony has survived the turbulent convulsions of civil war, Jacobite risings, the abolition of heritable jurisdictions, and the final dissolution of feudal land tenure itself.

This history traces the barony from its creation as a composite feudal holding—spanning territories from Stirlingshire to the Inner Hebrides—through the dramatic fortunes of the Campbell dynasty, the gradual erosion of baronial powers, and its ultimate transformation into an incorporeal heritable dignity under modern Scots law.

Understanding Scottish Feudal Baronies

Origins in the Feudal System

Scottish feudal baronies trace their origins to the 12th century, when King David I (r. 1124–1153) systematically introduced Norman feudal institutions to Scotland.[1] Having spent much of his youth at the court of his brother-in-law, Henry I of England, David recognised the administrative and military advantages of feudalism and imported the system wholesale upon his accession to the Scottish throne.

David granted baronies to Anglo-Norman and Flemish knights and aristocrats in exchange for ward—the obligation to provide the Crown with armed men and mounted knights when required. These grants were recorded in charters that erected lands in liberam baroniam (in free barony), directly from the Crown.[1] By the time of Robert I in the early 14th century, there were perhaps 200 to 300 baronies; by 1405 this had risen to 400, and by 1707, as James VI & I and his Stuart successors consolidated their joint kingdoms, the total approached 1,500.[1]

The Feudal Hierarchy: Superiority vs. Possession

One of the most commonly misunderstood aspects of Scottish feudal baronies concerns the relationship between the barony itself and actual ownership of the land. This confusion arises from the dual nature of feudal landholding under Scots law, which recognised two distinct forms of right over the same land:

Dominium Directum (Feudal Superiority)

This was the superior's right—in the case of a barony, held by the baron. The feudal superior held the ultimate title to the land but did not necessarily occupy or directly control it. The superior received feudal duties (feu-duties) from those who actually held the land below them in the feudal chain.

Dominium Utile (Beneficial Possession)

This was the right of the actual possessor or occupier of the land—the person who worked it, lived on it, and exercised practical control over it. This vassal held the land heritably and could sell or bequeath it, subject to the superior's rights.

These two rights could be—and frequently were—held by entirely different people. A baron might be the feudal superior of extensive lands without owning or occupying any of them directly. The institutional writer Lord Bankton explained that "the dignity of a barony" existed as a noble quality of the feudal grant, independent of whether the baron actually resided on or personally controlled the lands.[2]

This separation became particularly pronounced through subinfeudation—the practice whereby a baron could grant portions of baronial lands to vassals, who held them heritably and paid feu-duties to the baron, who in turn paid feudal obligations to the Crown. These vassals could themselves sub-infeudate with the superior's consent, creating a complex hierarchy of feudal relationships.

In extreme cases, a baron might subinfeudate all the lands of a barony, retaining only a small plot—sometimes no more than a particular stone in a corner of a field—as the caput baroniae (the head of the barony). These came to be called "telegraph-pole baronies" because the baron's only remaining interest might be charging wayleave fees to telegraph and telephone companies for erecting poles on the caput.[1]

Example: The Barony of Balvill By the 18th and 19th centuries, the Dukes of Argyll remained Barons of Balvill—holding the feudal superiority—while the actual lands of Balvill in Stirlingshire were possessed by the Stirling family, and later by James Macfarlane and his descendants. The Argylls received feu-duties from these possessors but did not occupy the lands themselves, residing instead at Inveraray Castle in Argyllshire.

What Baronies Conferred

Early grants of baronies typically contained the formula "soke and sake, toll and theame, infangthief and outfangthief." These Anglo-Norman and Anglo-Saxon terms, imported by David I, granted substantial jurisdictional powers:[1]

  • Soke and sake: The right to hold courts and administer justice, substituting local baronial jurisdiction for the King's national courts.
  • Toll and theame: The right to levy tolls on passage and sale of goods, and to hold courts concerning theft and retain the profits.
  • Infangthief and outfangthief: The right to try and punish (including execution) thieves caught within the barony's jurisdiction.

These jurisdictional privileges were symbolised by fossa et forca (pit and gallows)—the baron's right to capital punishment. Barons could also hold fairs and markets, create burghs of barony to control trade, and exact various feudal payments and services.[1]

However, it is crucial to understand that Scottish feudal barons were not peers. The lowest rank of the Scottish peerage was Lord of Parliament, entirely distinct from feudal barons. While originally all barons could attend Parliament as part of the Estate of the Barons, by 1428 there were so many that they began electing commissioners to represent them.[3] An Act of 1587 confirmed this arrangement and made clear that barons were no longer part of the nobility.[4] At the Union of Parliaments in 1707, feudal barons had no place in Westminster.

Creation of the Barony of Balvill (1630–1633)

Historical Context

The creation of the Barony of Balvill in 1630 must be understood against the backdrop of Charles I's troubled reign and the political ascendancy of the Campbell family in western Scotland. Archibald Campbell, Lord Lorne—the grantee—was the son of the 7th Earl of Argyll, a Catholic convert who had been forced into exile and declared a traitor in 1619.[5]

By 1630, Lord Lorne had secured royal favour and was consolidating Campbell power through strategic acquisitions and royal grants. The creation of Balvill formed part of a broader pattern: between 1625 and 1632, Charles I granted Lorne several composite baronies—Ardnamurchan (1625), Balvill (1630), and Gigha (1632)—each uniting dispersed Campbell holdings under a single baronial dignity.[6]

The 1630 Crown Charter

On 5 August 1630, at the Palace of Holyroodhouse in Edinburgh, King Charles I granted a Crown charter under the Great Seal of Scotland creating the Barony of Balvill.[7] The charter, recorded in the Register of the Great Seal as number 1623, granted the barony to:

Archibald Lord Lorne &c., and his heirs male and assignees whatsoever

The charter united various lands and jurisdictions previously held separately, incorporating them into a single barony. Crucially, the charter designated "the manor place of Baluill to be the principal messuage"—that is, the caput baroniae or head of the barony.[7] This was the legal anchor point of the barony, the place from which the feudal dignity derived its name and nominal seat.

The Composite Geography

What made the Barony of Balvill unusual was its composite and geographically dispersed nature. The charter incorporated lands in several distinct regions:

The Caput: Stirlingshire

The principal messuage and caput lay at Balvill (or Baluill, sometimes Balwill) in the Parish of Drymen, Stirlingshire. The name derives from the Scottish Gaelic baile, meaning "home," "farmstead," or "town."[5] The 1630 charter specifically describes:

The lands of Baluill extending to 5 merks of old extent with a manor place, lands of Woodend with forest, Ballairds Over and Nether with the millstream of Ballairds, with tenantries &c., by annexation in the barony of Gleneagles, parish of Drymen [7]

These lands had previously been part of the Barony of Gleneagles, held by the Haldane family, and were resigned by John Haldane of Gleneagles (with the consent of his wife, Catherine Wemyss) to allow their incorporation into the new barony.[7]

The charter also incorporated neighbouring lands: "lands of Calyemoir called Ross, Lurglorne, with tenantries &c., in the barony of Haldan, lands of Kepdowrie with tenantries &c., in the barony of Gleneagles; 5 merks of the lands of Auchreg Over and Nether, in the barony of Haldan."[7] All of these lay in or near Drymen, in what was then recorded as the "shire of Perth" but was actually Stirlingshire—the boundary between the two counties lay nearby and was often confused in period documents.

The Islands: Jura, Oronsay, and Colonsay

Far to the west, in the Inner Hebrides, the barony incorporated substantial island holdings. The charter granted:

The island of Oronsay extending to 5 merks of lands, 16 shillings 8 pennies of lands of Garvart in Colonsay, 8 shillings of Stronane in Jura, with mills and fishings, formerly pertaining to the priory of Oronsay... also the third part of 18 merks of lands of Queyneis [various named subdivisions], in the island of Mull, the island of Rum extending to 6 merks of old extent... 16 shillings 8 pennies of lands of Ardvanische, [and other named lands] in the island of Jura [7]

These island lands had come from various sources: some from the dissolved Priory of Oronsay following the Reformation; others from William Stirling of Auchyle; others from Lauchlane MacLaine of Coll and his son; and still others from Lauchlane MacLaine of Morvern and Archibald Campbell of Kilmun.[7]

The geographic separation between the caput in Stirlingshire and the island holdings over 100 miles to the west was not unusual for major Scottish noble families. The Campbells, as chiefs of Clan Campbell and (from 1457) Earls of Argyll, held extensive territories throughout western Scotland. Designating Balvill—more accessible from Edinburgh and the centres of royal power—as the caput of a barony that included remote island holdings reflected both practical governance considerations and the symbolic importance of having a mainland seat.

The Feudal Payments The charter specified different forms of reddendo (feudal payment) for different parts of the barony: £3 for Oronsay, various amounts for other portions in feu-ferme (cash payment), with doubling of the feu-ferme at the entry of heirs, and payment of stipends to ministers of churches where the lands lay.[7]

Parliamentary Confirmation (1633)

On 28 June 1633, the Scottish Parliament passed an Act in favour of Archibald Campbell, Lord Lorne, ratifying and confirming the 1630 charter.[6] The Act stated:

Our sovereign lord, with advice and consent of the three estates of this present parliament, ratifies and approves the three charters after-mentioned... the second of the said charters made and granted by his majesty under the great seal to the said Archibald, lord Lorne and his foresaids of the lands and others mentioned therein thereby united in a barony called the barony of Balavil [sic], of the date at Holyroodhouse, 5 August 1630 [6]

This parliamentary ratification—coming three years after the original charter—provided an additional layer of legal security. While the Crown charter alone was sufficient to create a barony, parliamentary confirmation made it exceptionally difficult for future monarchs or governments to challenge the grant's validity.

By 1633, Archibald Campbell had styled himself Lord Lorne for over a decade but would not inherit his father's earldom until 1638, when the 7th Earl died. In 1641, Charles I elevated him further, creating him 1st Marquess of Argyll—a dignity he would hold until his execution in 1661.

The Campbell Dynasty (1630–2025)

For 395 years, the Barony of Balvill remained continuously associated with the Campbell family and, from 1701, the Dukes of Argyll. This extraordinary continuity spanned periods of dramatic political upheaval, civil war, Jacobite risings, the Union of Parliaments, the abolition of heritable jurisdictions, and ultimately the end of feudal tenure itself.

Power and Peril: The Civil War Era

Archibald Campbell, 8th Earl and 1st Marquess of Argyll (c. 1607–1661)

The original grantee of Balvill, Archibald Campbell, succeeded his father as 8th Earl of Argyll in 1638. By this time, he had already assumed effective control of the vast Argyll estates and emerged as the most powerful nobleman in Scotland—not merely through land and title, but as chief of Clan Campbell, commanding personal loyalty from some 20,000 armed clansmen.[8]

Unlike his father, who had converted to Catholicism and died in exile, the 8th Earl became a fervent Presbyterian and the principal leader of the Covenanters—the movement that opposed Charles I's attempt to impose Anglican religious practices on Scotland. When civil war erupted across the three kingdoms in the 1640s, Argyll effectively governed Scotland.[8]

In 1641, Charles I created Argyll Marquess, hoping to secure his loyalty. Instead, Argyll allied with the English Parliament against the King, though he later opposed Charles I's execution in 1649—an act no Scot had desired. Following the King's death, Argyll supported bringing Charles II to Scotland, personally placing the crown on the young king's head at his Scottish coronation at Scone on 1 January 1651.

This proved a fatal misjudgment. At the Restoration of 1660, Charles II—who had never forgiven the humiliations imposed on him by the Covenanters during his time in Scotland—had Argyll arrested in London. Despite having supported the King's return, Argyll was convicted of treason and beheaded on the "Maiden" (an early form of guillotine) in Edinburgh on 27 May 1661.[5] His estates, including the Barony of Balvill, were forfeited to the Crown.

Archibald Campbell, 9th Earl of Argyll (1629–1685)

The 9th Earl presents one of the more complex figures in the barony's history. Unlike his father, he had been raised a Royalist, fostered with relatives and educated abroad to keep him away from Scotland's religious conflicts. He had even fought against the Covenanters at the Battle of Dunbar in 1650, where his regiment was nearly annihilated.

In 1663, two years after his father's execution, Charles II restored the 9th Earl to the title and estates, including Balvill.[5] The young Earl received a new charter of all his lands and hereditary offices in 1667, and for two decades served the Crown, including leading expeditions to suppress Highland unrest.

However, the accession of the Catholic James VII (James II of England) in 1685 proved disastrous. The 9th Earl was put on trial for treason on questionable charges, condemned, and his estates again forfeited. He escaped from Edinburgh Castle (reportedly disguised as a page by his stepdaughter) and fled to Holland, where he joined other exiles plotting against James.[5]

In 1685, Argyll launched an ill-fated invasion of Scotland in coordination with the Duke of Monmouth's rebellion in England. Both failed utterly. Argyll was captured, returned to Edinburgh, and executed on 30 June 1685—on the same scaffold where his father had died 24 years earlier. According to witnesses, Argyll declared the Maiden "the sweetest maiden he had ever kissed."[5] His estates were forfeited for the second time in less than 25 years.

Restoration and Vindication

The Glorious Revolution of 1688, which overthrew James VII and brought William of Orange and Mary to the throne, vindicated both executed Earls posthumously. On 5 June 1689, the Scottish Parliament passed an Act rescinding the forfeiture of the 9th Earl, declaring that:

The causing pursue and forfeit several persons, upon stretches of old and obsolete laws, upon frivolous and weak pretences, upon lame and defective probation, and particularly the late earl of Argyll, to the scandal and reproach of the justice of the nation, are contrary to law [9]

Archibald Campbell, 10th Earl of Argyll—eldest son of the 9th Earl—was restored to his father's and grandfather's honours and estates in 1690, including the Barony of Balvill.

Consolidation and Stability

Archibald Campbell, 10th Earl and 1st Duke of Argyll (1658–1703)

The 10th Earl had lived through his father's and grandfather's executions and the forfeitures that followed. Understandably, he devoted his career to securing stable relations with the Crown. Having supported William and Mary's accession, he became one of William's principal Scottish advisors.

The 10th Earl commanded the Earl of Argyll's Regiment of Foot, which was involved in one of the most controversial episodes in Scottish history: the killing of members of the MacIan MacDonalds of Glencoe on 13 February 1692. While the Earl himself played no direct role—the responsibility lay with John Dalrymple, Master of Stair, and ultimately King William, who signed the orders—the involvement of Campbell soldiers in what came to be called the "Massacre of Glencoe" has remained a source of historical controversy and clan enmity.[5]

In 1701, King William created the 10th Earl Duke of Argyll, finally securing the family's position at the highest rank of the Scottish nobility. From this point forward, the Barony of Balvill would be held by Dukes of Argyll. The 1st Duke died in 1703 and was buried at Kilmun Parish Church, beginning a tradition continued by most of his successors.

John Campbell, 2nd Duke of Argyll (1680–1743)

The 2nd Duke was principally a military man, having served with distinction in the War of the Spanish Succession and commanded British forces in Spain. In Scottish history, he is best remembered for commanding government forces against the Jacobites at the Battle of Sheriffmuir on 13 November 1715 during the first major Jacobite rising.[5]

The battle was inconclusive—both sides claimed victory—but it effectively ended the '15 Rising's chances of success. The Jacobite commander, the Earl of Mar, subsequently fled to France, and the Old Pretender (James Francis Edward Stuart) soon followed.

For his services, the 2nd Duke received English titles in addition to his Scottish honours, being created Duke of Greenwich in 1719. However, having no sons, these English titles died with him in 1743, while his Scottish titles—including the Barony of Balvill—passed to his younger brother.

Archibald Campbell, 3rd Duke of Argyll (1682–1761)

Styled Earl of Ilay until succeeding his brother in 1743, the 3rd Duke was the predominant Scottish political figure of his era. Appointed Keeper of the Great Seal of Scotland in 1733, he managed Scotland's political affairs for the British government for decades, earning the nickname "King of Scotland" for his influence.[5]

He was also a keen botanist and one of the founders and first governor of the Royal Bank of Scotland in 1727, an institution that continues to this day and whose banknotes still bear his likeness.[10] Despite his prominence, the 3rd Duke died without sons, and the dukedom passed to a more distant cousin.

The Later Dukes

From John Campbell, 4th Duke (1693–1770)—a distinguished military officer who interrogated Flora MacDonald after Bonnie Prince Charlie's escape—through the 13th Duke, the successive holders of Argyll maintained the Barony of Balvill as part of their extensive feudal holdings.

The 8th Duke (1823–1900) served as Secretary of State for India and was a noted scientist and author. The 9th Duke (1845–1914) married Princess Louise, daughter of Queen Victoria, and served as Governor General of Canada from 1878 to 1883. The 10th Duke (1872–1949) funded the famous bell tower at Inveraray in memory of Campbells who died in the World Wars.[5]

Throughout these centuries, while the Dukes of Argyll retained the feudal superiority of Balvill, the actual lands in Stirlingshire were possessed by others. By the 18th century, the Stirling family held the Balvill lands; by the 19th century, the Macfarlane family. The Dukes received feu-duties but did not occupy the property, residing instead at Inveraray Castle in Argyllshire.

The Erosion of Feudal Power (1747)

The jurisdictional powers that had once made baronies significant—the right to hold courts, administer justice, and execute criminals—came to an abrupt end in 1747. The Heritable Jurisdictions (Scotland) Act, passed by the Westminster Parliament in the aftermath of the 1745 Jacobite Rising and the Battle of Culloden, abolished all private hereditary jurisdictions in Scotland.[11]

While ostensibly aimed at clan chiefs whose jurisdictional powers had enabled them to raise armies for the Jacobite cause, the Act affected all Scottish heritors, including Lowland barons and even Hanoverian supporters like the Duke of Argyll. The Crown took control of appointing Sheriffs, and the role of Justiciar was transferred to the High Court of Justiciary.

Because these jurisdictions were recognised as private property under the Act of Union of 1707, the government paid compensation to those affected (except Jacobites, whose estates had been forfeited). A total of £152,000 was distributed, with John Campbell, 4th Duke of Argyll, receiving £25,000—a substantial sum reflecting the extensive jurisdictional rights he had held.[1]

After 1747, baronies retained their character as feudal dignities and sources of feu-duties, but the substance of baronial power—jurisdiction over people and administration of justice—had been removed. Baronies became increasingly symbolic, though they remained heritable and continued to be bought and sold.

The Modern Transformation (2000–2004)

By the late 20th century, Scotland remained unique in Europe for retaining feudal land tenure. While feudalism had been abolished elsewhere, the Scottish system—whereby land was held ultimately from the Crown through a chain of feudal superiors—persisted as a method of controlling urban development and collecting feudal payments.

In 1999, the Scottish Law Commission published a report recommending the abolition of feudal tenure as part of comprehensive land reform.[12] While the report's primary concern was land law, it necessarily addressed the question of what would happen to feudal baronies.

The Commission noted that baronies had become detached from any meaningful connection to jurisdiction or governance: "The dignity of baron would become a 'floating' right or privilege" no longer connected to an interest in land.[12] However, the Commission recommended against abolishing baronies entirely, instead proposing that they be preserved as incorporeal heritable property—legal rights that could be owned and transferred but had no connection to land.

The resulting Abolition of Feudal Tenure etc. (Scotland) Act 2000 received Royal Assent on 9 June 2000 and was brought into force on 28 November 2004.[13] The appointed day—Martinmas, a traditional Scottish term day—marked the end of feudalism in Scotland after nearly 900 years.

For the Barony of Balvill and all other Scottish baronies, the 2004 implementation meant:

  • The feudal relationship between superior and vassal was abolished. The Duke of Argyll's feudal superiority over the Balvill lands ended.
  • All land in Scotland became held outright by its possessor (dominium plenum), free of feudal obligations.
  • The barony itself—the dignity—was preserved as incorporeal heritable property, capable of being owned, transferred by assignation, and inherited.
  • The barony no longer carried any rights over land, jurisdiction over people, or entitlement to feudal payments.

This transformation fundamentally changed what a Scottish barony is. From 1630 to 2004, Balvill had been a feudal tenure—a way of holding land with associated rights and obligations. After 2004, it became purely a dignity—a recognised title of honour under Scots law, but one with no territorial or jurisdictional content.

Various registration systems emerged to record these dignities. The Scottish Barony Register maintains an official register of baronies, entries in which may be marked private if the holder chooses. Authoritative directories such as Debrett's also list recognised Scottish baronies.[14][15]

The 2025 Transfer

In 2025, after 395 years of continuous association with the Campbell family and the Dukes of Argyll, the dignity of the Barony of Balvill was assigned to Mark Hopking.

Under modern Scots law, baronies are transferred by deed of assignation—a formal legal document assigning the incorporeal right from the previous holder to the new one. The assignation was executed on 9 September 2025, transferring the barony from Torquhil Ian Campbell, 13th Duke of Argyll, to Mark Hopking.[16]

Following the assignation, the transfer was registered, and the barony now appears in recognised directories and registers. The Scottish Barony Register's official list includes Balvill, with the holder's details marked as private.[14] Debrett's directory of feudal baronies lists Mark Hopking as Baron of Balvill.[15]

The 2025 transfer represents a significant transition in the barony's long history—the first time since 1630 that it has passed outside the Campbell family. Yet it also demonstrates the continuity of Scots law: a dignity created by Crown charter in 1630, confirmed by Parliament in 1633, held through centuries of political upheaval, transformed by the abolition of feudalism in 2004, can still be recognised, transferred, and held under the law in 2025.

The current holder has expressed his understanding of the dignity as a form of custodianship—preserving a nearly 400-year-old legal and historical entity while recognising that its significance today lies in historical continuity rather than territorial jurisdiction or political power.
